# Introduction

Welcome to the HB Computer Security InfoSec Aide Memoire.

I have been working in Information Security \(or Cyber security if you are so inclined...\) for over a decade, and in that time I have arrived at the conclusion that it is impossible for **most** mere mortals to commit to memory, and recall at will, everything we need \(or are expected to know at the drop of a hat...\) to do our jobs. There is simply too much that we, as InfoSec specialists, are expected to know off by heart, often by those who have little to no understanding of the subject themselves.

We've all been there - worked for weeks, months, perhaps even years, to master our craft, be that as a network engineer, web application developer, system administrator, or any other discipline of Information Technology, and just as we get to the stage where we think we've got everything worked out, we're finally feeling like you know what we're doing...

![](.gitbook/assets/image%20%2843%29.png)

An upgrade alters the core functionality or behaviour of our stuff, and everything we've worked on is now broken, or the technology stack changes and it's all unfamiliar. Perhaps a sudden move into a different area of the business requires us to learn something entirely new, and the whole process begins again from scratch - I mean, "_you're an IT guy, its all the same right?_"...

Worse still is when you have to circle back around after **\[INSERT RANDOM AMOUNT OF WEEKS, MONTHS or YEARS HERE\]** to revisit something you haven't worked on in a LOOOOOOOONG time, and you are expected to pick right back up where you left off without a moments preparation. Have you ever found yourself uttering any of the following:

> "_What does that line of code mean... that I forgot to comment... like an idiot_"
>
> "_What is that command that I need to run again?_"
>
> "_Well it worked LAST time..._"
>
> "It's never done THAT before..."

I'm pretty sure I'm not alone...

Now, I'm not blessed with eidetic memory, and I've never been fond of people who profess to know it all. In my experience, such folk seem incapable of acknowledging that either:

1. They **DONT** know it all \(unfortunately very common\) and there is some _**uniqueness**_ ****in their work... 
2. They are unique in their super human abilities, and that most of us do not possess such [phenomenal cosmic powers](https://64.media.tumblr.com/57299949cd82da34428e1cd50b803d85/tumblr_inline_ozqth2eCbZ1txdber_1280.png)

Now of course, there are plenty of immensely talented, brilliant people out there who do not fall into the same category as the rest of us. I call them [Unicorns](https://i.pinimg.com/originals/9e/0e/dc/9e0edc3bf5589b737487f5680e2e9a46.gif), and I hope one day to `<joke>`capture one, study it, and replicate their powers for the greater good`</joke>`. However, in the meantime, I have to seek alternative options.

Before I go any further, I want to make a few statements:

1. It is perfectly ok to say "_I don't know_" - it is **FAR** better to acknowledge a knowledge gap and do your research than it is to guess 
2. It is the norm to have to look things up continuously; few of us can remember it all 
3. It is better to know the core concepts of what is possible and look up the implementation, than blindly remember commands without context

Those three points are why this Aide Memoire exists. Allow me to explain.

I am a firm believer that it is better to admit you don't know a thing than it is to blag it and f&\*k it all up. Often when I am working professionally, and I sense that not everyone in the room is on the same page, I will erroneously claim to not know what we are discussing so that we are forced as a collective to take a moment, ensuring we are all up to speed. This saves someone else the "embarrassment" of saying the much dreaded phrase "I don't know", and is often met with a few relieved faces. My intent here is two fold:

1. Remove the apparent stigma surrounding the act of admittance 
2. Disempowering those whose confidence greatly outweighs their capabilities - I am quite opposed to the "those who speak loudest are correct" notion...

I also think that its better to learn how things work from a high-level perspective, and then drill down into the implementation specifics afterwards, as opposed to following a process with no real understanding of why things are happening, merely that they are. To do so is a sure fire way to introduce bad practice, vulnerabilities, and make poor business decisions. Instead, I'd rather be able to follow a concept, or understand a business requirement, and in turn transform that understanding into sound implementation using continuously tested, verified, repeatable, documented methods.

So, I work on this Aide Memoire iteratively as I learn more, building up an ever expanding knowledge bank I can refer to when I work. In doing so, I remove my reliance on committing arbitrary commands to memory, and instead write my own guides and explanations for things that I can repeat, ensuring consistency in my work. Not only does this help me to solidify my understanding of the concepts, but I can also follow my own advice when I'm feeling a little absent minded.

I only hope that it useful to you, the reader. So, please enjoy my ramblings. 

Let's get started.
